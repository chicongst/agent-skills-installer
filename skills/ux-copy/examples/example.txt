# Example: /ux-copy

## User Input
Review and improve the copy on our subscription cancellation flow. Current copy feels
aggressive and guilt-trippy. We want to be honest and respectful. Here are the
current strings:

1. Cancel button label: "Cancel my subscription"
2. Confirmation modal title: "Are you sure you want to leave us?"
3. Modal body: "You'll lose access to all your premium features and your data may
   be deleted. Think carefully before proceeding."
4. Confirm button: "Yes, cancel it"
5. Cancel button in modal: "No, keep my subscription!"
6. Success message: "Your subscription has been cancelled. We're sad to see you go 😢"

## Skill Output

# UX Copy Review: Subscription Cancellation Flow

## Context
- **User moment**: Actively choosing to leave — already decided or exploring options
- **Emotional state**: Potentially frustrated, cost-conscious, or simply done with the product
- **Goal**: Be honest and respectful. Don't guilt-trip. Make cancellation easy (dark patterns erode trust and generate chargebacks).

---

## 1. Cancel Button Label

**Current**: "Cancel my subscription"
**Problem**: Fine — it's clear. No change needed.
**Keep as-is** ✅

---

## 2. Modal Title

**Current**: "Are you sure you want to leave us?"
**Problem**: Guilt-trip framing. "Leave us" is emotionally manipulative. Also, the
user clicked Cancel — asking "are you sure?" is expected, but the phrasing adds drama.

**Revised**: "Cancel your subscription?"
**Why better**: Neutral, direct. Confirms the action without emotional manipulation.

**Alternatives**:
- Minimal: "Cancel subscription"
- Informative: "Before you go — here's what changes"

---

## 3. Modal Body

**Current**: "You'll lose access to all your premium features and your data may be
deleted. Think carefully before proceeding."

**Problems**:
- "may be deleted" — vague and scary. Is data deleted or not? Be specific.
- "Think carefully before proceeding" — condescending. They have already thought.
- Fear-based framing designed to stop cancellation (dark pattern)

**Revised**: "Your subscription ends on [current period end date]. After that, your
account switches to the free plan — you'll keep your data and basic features."

**Why better**:
- Specific about what happens (no vague threats)
- Positive framing (you keep data, you keep basic access)
- Honest about the outcome so users can decide with real information

**Alternatives**:
- If data IS deleted: "Your account and data will be permanently deleted on [date]. Export your data before then."
- Minimal: "Access ends [date]. Your data is kept for 30 days."

---

## 4. Confirm Button

**Current**: "Yes, cancel it"
**Problem**: "Yes, cancel it" is informal and slightly flippant for a consequential action.

**Revised**: "Cancel subscription"
**Why better**: Specific, matches the action name consistently. No filler words.

**Alternatives**:
- "Confirm cancellation"
- "Cancel plan" (shorter, works on mobile)

---

## 5. Keep Subscription Button

**Current**: "No, keep my subscription!"
**Problem**: Exclamation mark feels forced. The "No," prefix makes it wordy.
Asymmetric button labels ("Yes, cancel it" vs "No, keep") create cognitive load.

**Revised**: "Keep subscription"
**Why better**: Parallel structure with confirm button. Clear. No manipulation.

**Alternatives**:
- "Never mind"
- "Go back"

---

## 6. Success Message

**Current**: "Your subscription has been cancelled. We're sad to see you go 😢"
**Problem**: Guilt after the action is done serves no purpose. User has cancelled — respect the decision. The crying emoji is especially manipulative post-cancellation.

**Revised**: "Subscription cancelled. Your access continues until [end date]."

**Why better**:
- Confirms the action
- Gives the user useful information (when access actually ends)
- No guilt, no emoji drama

**Alternatives**:
- "You're all set. Your plan ends on [date] — you can reactivate anytime."
- Minimal: "Cancelled. Access until [date]."

---

## Summary of All Changes

| Element | Before | After |
|---------|--------|-------|
| Modal title | "Are you sure you want to leave us?" | "Cancel your subscription?" |
| Modal body | Fear-based, vague data deletion threat | Specific, positive (you keep data) |
| Confirm button | "Yes, cancel it" | "Cancel subscription" |
| Keep button | "No, keep my subscription!" | "Keep subscription" |
| Success message | Guilt + 😢 emoji | Confirms action + gives useful end date |

## Consistency Notes
- Both modal buttons now use parallel noun-phrase structure ("Cancel subscription" / "Keep subscription")
- Remove "my" from all labels — UI copy uses imperative, not first-person possessive
